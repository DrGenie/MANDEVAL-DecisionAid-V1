MANDEVAL – Technical appendix

Methods, parameters and guidance for the vaccine mandate decision aid (Australia, France, Italy).

Version v1.0.0  |  Built Jan 2026

Overview

1. Purpose and scope

This appendix documents the main inputs, modelling assumptions and outputs used in MANDEVAL, a decision aid for exploring the consequences of different COVID-19 vaccine mandate designs in Australia, France and Italy.

MANDEVAL combines preference estimates from a discrete choice experiment (DCE) with simple epidemiological and costing inputs to provide:

  • Model-based predicted public support for a given mandate configuration.
  • Expected lives saved in the exposed population, based on a “lives saved per 100,000” input.
  • Monetary valuation of lives saved, using a value per life saved (for example a value of statistical life).
  • Implementation costs and resulting net benefit and benefit–cost ratios.
  • “Lives-saved equivalents” (MRS) to interpret preferences over mandate design features.
Non-technical

2. How the tabs fit together

2.1. Settings

The Settings tab defines the population, analysis horizon and value per life saved. These apply to all scenarios:

  • Analysis horizon: number of years during which costs and benefits are considered.
  • Population covered: number of people exposed to the mandate.
  • Value per life saved: a monetary value attached to one life saved, aligned with national regulatory guidance where possible.

2.2. Mandate configuration

The Configuration tab selects the country, outbreak scenario and design features of the mandate (scope, exemptions, coverage threshold, expected lives saved). The preference model then calculates the predicted probability that people would support this mandate over no mandate.

2.3. Costing

The Costing tab allows users to enter indicative implementation costs across several categories (digital systems, communications, enforcement, compensation, administration and other costs). These are summed into a total implementation cost.

2.4. Results

The Results tab combines preference-based support, epidemiological inputs and costs to produce:

  • Total lives saved in the exposed population.
  • Monetary value of lives saved.
  • Total implementation cost, net benefit and benefit–cost ratio.
  • Lives-saved equivalents (MRS) that interpret changes in mandate features in terms of “equivalent lives saved per 100,000 people”.

Status chips indicate whether predicted support and the benefit–cost ratio appear high, medium or low, based on transparent thresholds.

The Saved scenarios tab stores complete scenarios (settings, configuration, costs and results) for comparison and export, while the Briefings & AI prompts tab helps users translate scenarios into short policy briefings or prompts for Copilot / ChatGPT.

Interpretation

3. Predicted public support

MANDEVAL reports “predicted public support” as the model-based probability that a respondent would choose a given mandate over an opt-out (no mandate) option, conditional on the country and outbreak frame.

The underlying utility functions are:

  • U(mandate): utility from a mandate with specific scope, exemptions, coverage and lives saved.
  • U(opt-out): utility from the “no mandate” option.

For a given vector of random preferences β, the model­based probability of choosing the mandate is:
P(mandate | β) = exp(U(mandate; β)) / [exp(U(mandate; β)) + exp(U(opt-out; β))].

In MANDEVAL, preferences β are assumed to follow a multivariate normal distribution, estimated separately for each country and outbreak frame. The predicted probability used in the tool is the average of P(mandate | β) over 1,000 draws from this distribution, using a fixed random seed for reproducibility.

Interpretation: a predicted support of 65% means that, under the stated conditions and extrapolating from the DCE, around two thirds of respondents would choose the mandate over no mandate. It is not a forecast of compliance or turnout and should be interpreted as an indicative acceptability measure.

Methods

4. Discrete choice experiment and mixed logit models

4.1. Attributes and levels

The DCE asked respondents to choose between two mandate options and an opt-out alternative. Mandates were described using:

  • Scope (high-risk occupations only vs all occupations & public spaces).
  • Exemption policy (medical only; medical + religious; medical + religious + personal belief).
  • Coverage threshold to lift the mandate (50%, 70% or 90% of the population vaccinated).
  • Expected lives saved per 100,000 people (10, 25, 35 or 40).

4.2. Mixed logit specification

For each country (Australia, France, Italy) and outbreak frame (mild / endemic, severe), a mixed logit (random parameters logit) model was estimated with normally distributed random coefficients:

  • ASC for Policy A (mandate alternative).
  • ASC for opt-out (no mandate).
  • Dummy variables for scope, exemptions and coverage threshold levels.
  • A continuous coefficient for expected lives saved per 100,000 people.

The tool uses the estimated means and standard deviations of these random coefficients to generate 1,000 draws from a multivariate normal distribution (using independent normals for each parameter). For each draw, utilities for the mandate and opt-out options are computed at the chosen configuration, and the logit probability of supporting the mandate is calculated. The mean of these probabilities across draws is reported as “predicted public support”.

Costs & benefits

5. Cost taxonomy and value per life saved

5.1. Cost categories

The costing framework in MANDEVAL is designed to mirror standard components used in regulatory impact analyses for vaccine mandates and similar public health measures. It includes:

  • Digital systems & infrastructure (IT platforms, certificates, registries, integration).
  • Communications & public information (campaigns, materials, stakeholder engagement).
  • Enforcement & compliance (inspection, enforcement staff, penalties administration).
  • Adverse-event monitoring & compensation (pharmacovigilance and compensation schemes).
  • Administration & programme management (design, legal, coordination, oversight).
  • Other mandate-specific costs (residual category for context-specific items).

Users can adapt these values based on country-specific data, financial reports or regulatory impact assessments.

5.2. Value per life saved

The tool applies a single value per life saved to monetise the estimated lives saved. In practice, this is often anchored in:

  • A value of statistical life (VSL) used in national regulatory guidance.
  • A value of a statistical life-year (VSLY), where appropriate for age-structured analyses.
  • Programme-specific willingness-to-pay thresholds (for example cost-effectiveness thresholds used in health technology assessment).

Users should align the value per life saved with relevant national or institutional guidance, and document the chosen value and source when using MANDEVAL in formal policy analysis.

  • Settings: population = 5,000,000; value per life saved = EUR 5,000,000; horizon = 1 year.

The tool translates this into a total lives saved figure, monetary benefit of lives saved, total implementation cost, net benefit, benefit–cost ratio and predicted support. Status chips and charts highlight whether support and BCR appear high, medium or low, given the assumptions.

Governance

7. Versioning and quality control

MANDEVAL has been structured to make updates transparent and auditable. The footer in the main application shows the version number and build date. This appendix includes a short changelog.

7.1. Internal quality checks

Before publication, a small grid of test scenarios is defined and evaluated using both MANDEVAL and the original estimation environment (for example MATLAB or R). Predicted support, lives saved, benefit–cost metrics and lives-saved equivalents are cross-checked to ensure the web implementation reproduces the reference calculations up to expected rounding differences.
