MANDEVAL Technical Appendix

This appendix explains how the MANDEVAL decision aid works, in two layers: a non-technical layer for policy users and a more technical layer for analysts.

1. Overview (non-technical)

MANDEVAL is a decision aid that links three main ingredients:

  • Public preferences from a discrete choice experiment (DCE) on vaccine mandates.
  • Epidemiological benefit expressed as “expected lives saved per 100,000 people”.
  • Indicative economic value combining the value of lives saved with implementation costs.

For each configuration (country, outbreak scenario and mandate design), the tool:

  • Predicts the probability of public support for that mandate,
  • Converts the “lives saved per 100,000” input into total lives saved and a monetary value, and
  • Compares this value with implementation costs entered by the user.

2. Main tabs and what they do

  • Introduction Explains the purpose of the tool, who it is for, and how to use it in five simple steps.
  • Mandate design features Lists the DCE attributes (scope, exemptions, coverage, lives saved) and their levels in plain language.
  • Settings & assumptions Sets the analysis horizon, population, currency and value-per-life metric (e.g. VSL).
  • Configuration Lets users choose a country, outbreak scenario and mandate design. This drives the model-based support calculation.
  • Costing & implementation Collects indicative costs across key components (digital passes, communications, enforcement, etc.).
  • Results Shows lives saved, monetary benefits, costs, BCR, predicted public support and “lives-saved equivalents” (MRS).
  • Saved scenarios & exports Stores scenarios in the browser, compares them and exports data for reports.
  • Briefings & AI Pre-populates a briefing template and an AI-ready prompt for tools like Copilot or ChatGPT.

3. How each tab works (non-technical view)

3.1 Mandate design features

The Mandate design features tab summarises the attributes and levels used in the DCE. Each attribute can be thought of as a “dial” that can be turned to design a mandate. The four core dials are:

  • Scope of mandate: who is covered (only high-risk workers vs. everyone in public spaces).
  • Exemptions: how narrow or broad exemptions are (medical only vs. medical + religious + personal belief).
  • Coverage requirement: vaccination threshold required to lift the mandate (50%, 70% or 90%).
  • Estimated lives saved: expected lives saved per 100,000 if the mandate is introduced.

In the DCE, people saw different combinations of these features and chose between two mandates and an opt-out option. This reveals how strongly people care about each feature.

3.2 Settings & assumptions

The Settings & assumptions tab fixes the “background numbers” that are reused across scenarios:

  • Analysis horizon: the time period over which costs and benefits are considered (e.g. 1 year).
  • Population exposed: the number of people covered by the mandate (e.g. 1 million high-risk workers).
  • Currency label: how monetary values are labelled (e.g. A$, €, local currency units).
  • Value per life saved: a VSL-type number that converts lives saved into monetary terms.

Changing these settings will update all downstream calculations. The tool does not enforce any particular value – it makes assumptions explicit, so they can be discussed and, if needed, revised.

3.3 Configuration

The Configuration tab is where a specific mandate is defined:

  • Choose country (Australia, France or Italy).
  • Choose outbreak scenario (mild/endemic vs. severe outbreak).
  • Set the scope, exemptions and coverage threshold according to the DCE levels.
  • Set the expected lives saved per 100,000 using the slider.

When you click “Apply configuration”, the tool:

  • Uses the mixed logit estimates for the chosen country and outbreak frame.
  • Simulates many “draws” of preferences to reflect heterogeneity in the population.
  • Calculates the predicted probability that the mandate is supported, versus opting out.

When you click “Save scenario”, the configuration and results are stored in your browser and appear in the Saved scenarios & exports tab.

3.4 Costing & implementation

The Costing & implementation tab aggregates user-entered costs across key components. These are indicative and should be refined with local data. This feeds into the net benefit and BCR calculations.

3.5 Results (including MRS)

The Results tab brings together three pillars:

  • Lives and monetary benefits: total lives saved and their monetary valuation.
  • Costs and BCR: total implementation cost, net benefit and benefit–cost ratio.
  • Predicted public support: probability that the mandate is chosen over opt-out in the DCE.

The “lives-saved equivalent” section then interprets how strongly people care about mandate design features in terms of lives saved. It asks: “how many expected lives saved per 100,000 would compensate for changing this attribute?”

Example intuition: If making exemptions stricter (e.g. removing personal-belief exemptions) reduces utility, this might show up as a positive lives-saved equivalent value: “this change feels as costly as losing 2 lives saved per 100,000 people”.

3.6 Saved scenarios & exports; Briefings & AI

The Saved scenarios tab keeps a local “portfolio” of options. The export functions allow users to generate:

  • CSV/Excel-readable files for more detailed analysis,
  • Plain-text summaries for briefing documents, and
  • AI-ready prompts that can be used to draft tailored communications.

4. Technical details (for analysts)

4.1 Mixed logit model and predicted support

The underlying preference models are mixed logit models estimated separately for each country (Australia, France, Italy) and outbreak frame (mild vs. severe). For each of these six combinations, the model estimates:

  • Mean coefficients for: ASC – Policy A ASC – Opt-out Scope (all vs. high-risk) Exemptions (med+rel, med+rel+pers) Coverage thresholds (70%, 90%) Lives saved per 100,000
  • Standard deviations for each of these random coefficients.

In the tool, these are used as follows:

  • For a given country and outbreak scenario, the mean and standard deviation for each coefficient are loaded.
  • For each configuration, the tool draws 1,000 simulated preference vectors by sampling from a normal distribution around each mean with the estimated standard deviation (independent draws for each coefficient).
  • For each draw, it forms a utility for the mandate alternative and a utility for the opt-out alternative:

Mandate utility (per draw):

  • Start from the policy-alternative intercept.
  • Add the coefficient for the chosen scope (if population-wide).
  • Add the coefficient for the chosen exemption policy.
  • Add the coefficient for the chosen coverage threshold (70% or 90%; 50% is the reference).
  • Add the lives-saved coefficient multiplied by the configured “lives saved per 100,000”.

Opt-out utility (per draw):

  • Use only the opt-out intercept from that draw.

For each draw, the probability of supporting the mandate is computed using a two-alternative logit formula:

  • The support probability is then averaged across all 1,000 draws to obtain a model-based estimate of public support.

4.2 Lives saved and monetary benefits

The epidemiological benefit parameter in the DCE is the expected number of lives saved per 100,000 people. In the tool:

  • The user sets L = expected lives saved per 100,000.
  • The exposed population is P.
  • Expected lives saved in the covered population are L × P / 100,000.
  • Monetary benefits are (L × P / 100,000) × V, where V is the value per life saved.

The value per life can be interpreted as a VSL, a value per life-year (VSLY) multiplied by expected years of life saved, or another locally agreed metric.

4.3 Costs, net benefit and BCR

Costs are entered as level sums across components such as digital systems, communications, enforcement and compensation schemes. These are treated as one-off (undiscounted) costs over the chosen horizon:

  • Total cost is the sum of all components.
  • Net benefit is monetary benefit minus total cost.
  • BCR is monetary benefit divided by total cost (if total cost > 0).

4.4 Lives-saved equivalents (MRS)

The lives-saved equivalent calculations translate preference strength for non-lives attributes into the same units as the lives-saved attribute. For each attribute change (e.g. moving from high-risk scope to population-wide scope), the tool uses the ratio of the attribute coefficient to the lives-saved coefficient to obtain a number of lives per 100,000 people that would have the same effect on utility.

Interpretation:

  • A positive lives-saved equivalent means the change reduces acceptability: it would require extra lives saved to offset this loss in utility.
  • A negative lives-saved equivalent means the change increases acceptability: it is as attractive as gaining additional lives saved.

5. Worked example (non-technical)

Suppose we configure the following mandate:

  • Country: Italy; outbreak: severe.
  • Scope: all occupations & public spaces.
  • Exemptions: medical + religious.
  • Coverage threshold: 70% of the population.
  • Expected lives saved: 25 per 100,000.
  • Population covered: 1,000,000 people.
  • Value per life saved: 3,000,000 in local currency units.

The tool will:

  • Simulate 1,000 preference draws from the Italian severe mixed logit model.
  • For each draw, compute the probability of supporting this mandate versus opting out.
  • Average these probabilities to report model-based public support (e.g. “about 65%”).
  • Compute total lives saved: 25 × 1,000,000 / 100,000 = 250 lives saved.
  • Compute monetary benefit: 250 × 3,000,000 = 750,000,000 in local currency units.
  • Combine this with entered costs to compute net benefit and BCR.
  • Translate the impact of changing scope, exemptions or coverage into lives-saved equivalents using the model coefficients.

All numbers produced by MANDEVAL are indicative and depend on the chosen assumptions. For formal regulatory impact analysis, the underlying data, code and parameters should be reviewed, and local costing data should be integrated.
