MANDEVAL – COVID-19 Vaccine Mandate Decision Aid

Uses discrete choice experiment (DCE) evidence and simple cost–benefit logic to compare alternative COVID-19 vaccine mandate designs in Australia, France and Italy.

Evidence base “No Jab, No Access?” DCE study
Intended users Policy teams, technical advisers, partners
Status Decision-aid prototype

What MANDEVAL does

MANDEVAL is an interactive decision aid that links public preferences, epidemiological benefits and indicative costs for alternative COVID-19 vaccine mandate designs in Australia, France and Italy.

  • Preferences: Uses mixed logit (MXL) estimates from a discrete choice experiment (DCE) conducted in three countries under mild and severe outbreak frames.
  • Benefits: Converts “expected lives saved per 100,000 people” into total lives saved and monetary benefits using a value-per-life metric.
  • Costs: Aggregates user-entered implementation costs (e.g. digital systems, communications, enforcement, compensation funds) to produce net benefit and benefit–cost ratio (BCR).
  • Feasibility: Uses model-based predicted public support as a proxy for acceptability and implementation feasibility.

How to use the tool

  1. Review mandate attributes. In the Mandate design features tab, review the DCE attributes and levels used to describe vaccine mandates.
  2. Set your assumptions. In the Settings & assumptions tab, choose the analysis horizon, population exposed, currency, and value-per-life metric.
  3. Configure a mandate. In the Configuration tab, choose a country, outbreak scenario and mandate design (scope, exemptions, coverage threshold and expected lives saved).
  4. Enter costs. In the Costing & implementation tab, enter indicative costs for each main cost component (or leave at zero if costs are unknown).
  5. Interpret the results. In the Results tab, compare net benefit, BCR and predicted public support, and interpret marginal rates of substitution (MRS) expressed in “lives-saved equivalents”.
  6. Save and export scenarios. Use the Saved scenarios & exports tab to store, compare and export scenarios for briefings, emails or reports.

MANDEVAL is not a forecasting oracle: it organises available evidence and assumptions to aid transparent comparison of policy options. All results depend on the inputs and should be interpreted as indicative, not exact.

Attributes and levels in the vaccine mandate preference study

The DCE asked members of the public in Australia, France and Italy to choose between alternative COVID-19 vaccine mandate designs and an opt-out (no mandate) option. Each mandate was described using the attributes below.

Level Plain-language description

All occupations & public spaces Everyone working or entering public venues (e.g. cinemas, malls, restaurants, public transport) must be vaccinated.

Level Plain-language description

Medical + religious Exemptions allowed for medical reasons or documented religious beliefs (verification required).
Medical + religious + personal belief Exemptions allowed for medical reasons, religious beliefs or strong personal objections (broader opt-out).

Analysis settings

Suggested reference values (to be adapted to current regulatory guidance and price levels):

  • Australia: VSL around A$5–6 million (transport and regulatory impact analyses).
  • France: VSL around €3 million (French and EU transport/environment guidelines).
  • Italy: VSL around €3.0–3.3 million (EU road-safety and environment guidelines).

The tool does not enforce these values – they should be set explicitly by the user and documented in any briefing.

“Lives-saved equivalent” interpretation (MRS)

This section translates preferences for mandate design features into a “lives-saved equivalent” scale. For each attribute, the model estimates how many expected lives saved per 100,000 people would compensate for changing that attribute, keeping overall utility constant.

Attribute change Lives-saved equivalent
(per 100,000)		 Intuitive interpretation

Configure a mandate to see how changes in scope, exemptions or coverage compare to changes in expected lives saved. Positive values indicate changes that reduce acceptability (they would need extra lives saved to offset them); negative values indicate changes that increase acceptability (they are equivalent to additional lives saved in terms of public support).
